About ALK

ALK (anaplastic lymphoma kinase) is a protein that plays a role in the development and function of nerve cells in the brain. It is a receptor tyrosine kinase that is activated by binding to specific ligands. ALK was originally identified as a gene that is fused with the NPM1 gene in anaplastic large cell lymphoma (ALCL), a type of non-Hodgkin lymphoma. This fusion protein, known as NPM-ALK, has constitutive kinase activity and promotes cell proliferation and survival.

In addition to ALCL, ALK fusions have also been identified in a subset of non-small cell lung cancer (NSCLC) cases, particularly in younger patients with adenocarcinoma histology who are non-smokers or light smokers. The most common ALK fusion partner in NSCLC is the echinoderm microtubule-associated protein-like 4 (EML4) gene, resulting in the EML4-ALK fusion protein.

Targeting ALK with specific inhibitors has been shown to be an effective treatment strategy in ALK-positive ALCL and NSCLC. Several ALK inhibitors have been developed and approved for the treatment of ALK-positive NSCLC, including crizotinib, ceritinib, alectinib, brigatinib, and lorlatinib. These drugs inhibit the activity of the ALK kinase, leading to reduced proliferation and survival of ALK-positive cancer cells. However, the development of resistance to ALK inhibitors remains a challenge, and ongoing research is focused on identifying strategies to overcome this resistance and improve outcomes for patients with ALK-positive cancers.
