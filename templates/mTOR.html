About mTOR

Aberrant activation of mTOR signaling has been implicated in the development and progression of several types of cancer, including breast cancer, prostate cancer, lung cancer, renal cell carcinoma, and others. As a result, mTOR inhibitors have been developed as a class of anticancer drugs.

Two main classes of mTOR inhibitors have been developed: rapalogs and ATP-competitive inhibitors. Rapalogs, such as everolimus and temsirolimus, bind to the FKBP12 protein and inhibit the activity of mTOR complex 1 (mTORC1), resulting in reduced protein synthesis and cell proliferation. ATP-competitive inhibitors, such as sapanisertib and vistusertib, directly inhibit the kinase activity of mTOR and target both mTORC1 and mTOR complex 2 (mTORC2).

Several mTOR inhibitors have been approved for the treatment of various types of cancer. For example, everolimus is approved for the treatment of advanced renal cell carcinoma, pancreatic neuroendocrine tumors, and breast cancer, while temsirolimus is approved for the treatment of advanced renal cell carcinoma. Other mTOR inhibitors are currently being evaluated in clinical trials for the treatment of different types of cancer.

While mTOR inhibitors have shown promise in the treatment of cancer, they can also have side effects, such as immunosuppression, metabolic alterations, and others. Ongoing research is aimed at developing more effective and targeted mTOR inhibitors, as well as identifying biomarkers to predict response to treatment and guide patient selection.
